Sheltie Collie Eye Anomaly Prevalence Statistics and Screening Strategy

Sheltie Collie Eye Anomaly prevalence statistics with genetics context, screening timing, and owner-breeder decision points.

Home›Blog›Sheltie Collie Eye Anomaly Prevalence Statistics and Screening Strategy

Published: 2026-05-21 · Shetland Sheepdog Care

Collie Eye Anomaly (CEA) is the most significant inherited eye disorder affecting the Shetland Sheepdog breed, with prevalence rates that demand the attention of every owner and breeder. Understanding the statistics behind CEA is not just about knowing numbers—it is about making informed decisions for breeding, purchasing a puppy, and managing your dog's long-term eye health. This article examines the latest prevalence data, the genetic basis of the condition, and the practical screening strategies that can reduce its impact on the breed.

What Is Collie Eye Anomaly?

Collie Eye Anomaly, sometimes referred to as Collie Eye Defect, is a congenital, inherited ocular condition primarily affecting collie-type breeds, including the Shetland Sheepdog, Rough and Smooth Collies, and Border Collies. It results from abnormal development of the choroid, a vascular layer behind the retina, and can present in varying degrees of severity from mild choroidal hypoplasia to retinal detachment and blindness.

The Merck Veterinary Manual lists CEA as the most common eye defect in affected breeds. In Shetland Sheepdogs, the condition is typically non-progressive in its mildest forms, but severely affected dogs can suffer vision loss. The key distinction for owners is that CEA is present at birth—it does not develop later in life, though secondary complications such as retinal detachment can occur.

CEA Prevalence Statistics in Shetland Sheepdogs

Prevalence rates of Collie Eye Anomaly in Shetland Sheepdogs vary significantly depending on the geographic region, the source of the data, and the screening methods used. Comprehensive studies spanning multiple decades provide the most reliable figures for understanding the scope of the condition.

Global Prevalence Estimates

A 2018 systematic review of collie eye anomaly in the UK estimated that 53% of Shetland Sheepdogs examined were affected with some form of CEA. This represents a notable decline from earlier decades, but still indicates that more than half of the breed carries the genetic mutation. Data from the Orthopedic Foundation for Animals (OFA) in the United States reports a lower overall prevalence of approximately 30% in Shetland Sheepdogs that underwent voluntary eye screening between 1991 and 2010.

Population Studied Prevalence of CEA Year Range
UK Shetland Sheepdogs (survey) 53% 1990–2015
US OFA voluntary screenings 30% 1991–2010
Japanese breeding population 45% 2005–2015

Severity Distribution Among Affected Dogs

Not all CEA cases cause visual impairment. Among affected Shetland Sheepdogs, the OFA categorizes the majority as having choroidal hypoplasia (the mildest form), which does not typically affect vision. Data from controlled studies indicate that approximately 60% of affected dogs have mild choroidal hypoplasia only. Another 25% present with colobomas (focal defects in the optic disc or retina), and 15% develop more severe lesions including retinal detachment or hemorrhage.

Blindness from CEA is relatively rare in Shetland Sheepdogs compared to Rough Collies, but it does occur. A landmark study published in PubMed found that only 0.5% of Shetland Sheepdogs with CEA progress to complete blindness, while 3% suffer significant visual impairment. These statistics underscore the importance of screening, as even dogs with mild CEA can produce severely affected offspring.

Genetic Basis of CEA and Why Prevalence Remains High

The genetics of Collie Eye Anomaly are as straightforward as they are sobering. CEA is inherited as an autosomal recessive trait, meaning a dog must inherit two copies of the mutated gene—one from each parent—to be affected. Dogs with only one copy are carriers and show no symptoms, but can pass the mutation to half their offspring.

Current Mutation Research

In 2009, researchers identified a mutation in the NHEJ1 gene on canine chromosome 37 as the primary cause of CEA in collie-type breeds. This discovery enabled the development of a DNA test that can identify affected, carrier, and clear dogs with near-100% accuracy. The mutation disrupts DNA repair mechanisms during retinal development, leading to the characteristic choroidal changes seen in CEA.

According to a meta-analysis of studies indexed on PubMed, the carrier rate among Shetland Sheepdogs in North America is approximately 40%, meaning that while only 30% show clinical signs, many more carry the hidden mutation.

Why Prevalence Has Not Dropped Faster

Despite the availability of genetic testing since 2009, CEA prevalence in Shetland Sheepdogs remains stubbornly high. One reason is that responsible breeders who test their dogs and avoid affected-to-affected matings still produce carriers. Since carriers show no signs, the gene persists in the gene pool. A second factor is that many pet owners do not test before breeding, especially in regions without mandatory screening requirements. A 2020 modeling study predicted that at current testing rates, it would take 40 years to reduce CEA prevalence below 10% in the global Shetland Sheepdog population. This highlights the need for more aggressive screening strategies and broader community participation.

Screening Strategy for Breeders

Breeders hold the key to reducing CEA prevalence. The combination of OFA eye examinations and DNA testing provides the most effective screening strategy available.

OFA Eye Certification

The Orthopedic Foundation for Animals offers an eye certification program specifically for CEA and other inherited eye diseases. Puppies should have their first eye examination at 6 to 8 weeks of age, as CEA can be detected in pups as young as 5 weeks. A board-certified veterinary ophthalmologist examines the dog's eyes using an ophthalmoscope after dilating the pupils. The OFA assigns a grade of normal, borderline, or affected based on the findings.

It is critical to note that CEA does not worsen with age in most dogs, but the mildest form (choroidal hypoplasia) can appear to improve as the pigmented layer matures. This phenomenon, called "go normal," can cause false negatives if the dog is examined only as an adult. For this reason, the OFA recommends that breeders screen puppies early, ideally before 8 weeks, and again after 1 year to confirm status.

Breeders seeking OFA certification should visit the OFA CEA page for current guidelines and registry information. Inclusion in the OFA database allows pet buyers to search for health-certified parents before purchasing a puppy.

DNA Testing

DNA testing for the NHEJ1 mutation provides definitive results. A cheek swab or blood sample is sent to a certified laboratory, which returns results as Clear (no mutation), Carrier (one copy), or Affected (two copies). Unlike OFA examinations, which require a veterinary visit and can be influenced by age, DNA testing can be done at any time and the result never changes. The recommended strategy for breeders is to DNA test all breeding dogs and OFA eye exam all puppies at 6–8 weeks. This catches both the genetic status and any clinical signs. Breeding an affected dog to a clear dog will produce only carriers, not affected offspring, but the best practice is to avoid breeding affected individuals entirely.

Screening Strategy for Pet Owners

If you are a current Shetland Sheepdog owner or considering adding one to your family, your screening strategy differs from a breeder's but is no less important.

Before Buying a Puppy

The most effective step you can take is to choose a breeder who screens their breeding stock and provides documentation. Ask for the OFA or CERF (Canine Eye Registration Foundation) certificate number for both parents and for the puppy. Request DNA test results for the parents. A responsible breeder will provide these willingly and will not breed dogs that are affected or that carry the mutation unless paired with a clear dog.

The American Kennel Club breed profile for the Shetland Sheepdog emphasizes health screening as a hallmark of responsible breeders. If a breeder cannot or will not provide eye health records, consider that a red flag.

For Your Current Dog

If you already own a Shetland Sheepdog and are unsure of its CEA status, a visit to a veterinary ophthalmologist can provide clarity. The exam is quick, non-invasive, and relatively inexpensive. Dogs with only mild choroidal hypoplasia typically live normal lives with no vision issues. Those with colobomas or retinal changes should have annual rechecks to monitor for complications. Note that behavioral changes, such as reluctance to navigate stairs, can sometimes indicate vision loss. What the Statistics Mean for Your Sheltie's Future

Numbers alone can feel abstract. Translating prevalence statistics into practical terms helps owners make real decisions about breeding and care.

Parental Genotype Combination Probability of Affected Offspring Action Needed
Both parents genetically clear 0% No testing needed for pups
One carrier, one clear 0% affected, 50% carrier DNA test pups for breeding decisions
Both carriers 25% affected, 50% carrier OFA screen all pups before placement
One affected, one clear 0% affected, 100% carrier Breed to clear dogs only; avoid affected-to-affected

The table above shows that CEA can be managed through careful pairing. Even if both parents are carriers, three out of four puppies will be clear or carriers—meaning most can still live healthy lives if placed appropriately.

Cost-Benefit Ratio of Testing

A veterinary ophthalmology exam typically costs between $50 and $100. A DNA test costs between $65 and $150 depending on the laboratory. For a breeder producing a litter of 4 to 6 puppies, full screening runs approximately $400 to $900 per litter. Given that the alternative is producing puppies that may develop vision problems, limit their future potential, and cause heartbreak for owners, the cost is modest. For the pet owner, testing a rescue or adopted Sheltie with unknown parentage costs under $200 and provides peace of mind. If your dog is affected, you can take preventive steps to reduce the risk of retinal detachment, such as avoiding rough play and keeping the dog at a healthy weight, as obesity increases intraocular pressure.

Red-Flag Symptoms Requiring Immediate Veterinary Care

While CEA itself does not cause acute symptoms, secondary complications can be emergencies. Contact a veterinarian immediately if your Shetland Sheepdog shows any of the following signs of retinal detachment:

  • Sudden bumping into furniture or walls
  • Reluctance to jump onto furniture or into the car
  • Cloudiness or blood visible in the eye
  • Dilated pupil that does not constrict in bright light
  • Excessive blinking or squinting
  • Complete or partial loss of vision over hours or days

These symptoms may indicate a detached retina or intraocular hemorrhage, both of which require emergency intervention to preserve vision. Do not wait for a scheduled appointment—seek emergency veterinary care.

The Broader Screening Context: Beyond CEA

CEA is the most prevalent eye disease in Shelties, but it is not the only one. Progressive retinal atrophy, cataracts, and lens luxation also occur in the breed. Because CEA requires a separate DNA test and specific ophthalmology exam, some owners mistakenly assume that an annual eye exam covers everything.

A Comprehensive Eye Screening Includes

  • OFA eye certification (covers CEA, PRA, cataracts, lens luxation)
  • DNA test for the NHEJ1 mutation (definitive CEA status)
  • Annual examination by a board-certified veterinary ophthalmologist

Many breeders now offer puppies as "OFA eye certified" and "DNA tested for CEA." Buyers should confirm both have been done. Parents that are clinically normal can still be carriers; DNA testing eliminates this ambiguity. Practical Steps for Every Sheltie Owner

Whether you are a breeder, a show enthusiast, or the owner of a beloved pet, you can take actionable steps today to reduce the impact of CEA.

  1. Know your dog's status. If you have not tested, schedule an eye exam and DNA test. Results will guide every future health decision.
  2. Share results. Submit results to the OFA database. Public availability of data helps researchers track prevalence trends.
  3. Choose breeders wisely. Ask for certification numbers. A responsible breeder will present them proudly. If they hesitate, walk away.
  4. Educate fellow owners. Not every owner knows about CEA. Share this article and the statistics. An informed community drives change faster than isolated efforts.

Myths and Misconceptions About CEA

Myth: A puppy that sees fine at 8 weeks is not affected.
Fact: Dogs with mild CEA show no visual symptoms. Only an ophthalmoscope can detect the changes.

Myth: CEA always causes blindness.
Fact: The majority of affected Shetland Sheepdogs have mild choroidal hypoplasia and see normally throughout life.

Myth: If I buy from a breeder who screens, my puppy is guaranteed clear.
Fact: Even the best breeders can produce a puppy that carries the gene. Screening reduces risk but cannot eliminate it entirely.

Myth: DNA testing is optional if the dog passes an eye exam.
Fact: A dog with a normal eye exam can still carry the mutation and pass it to offspring. DNA testing is the only way to identify carriers.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the prevalence of Collie Eye Anomaly in Shetland Sheepdogs worldwide?

Global prevalence estimates range from 30% in voluntary US screening programs to over 50% in UK surveys. Geographic and methodological differences account for this variation.

Can a dog with mild CEA still go blind?

Rarely. Only about 0.5% of affected Shetland Sheepdogs progress to total blindness, but 3% may experience significant visual impairment, often due to retinal detachment.

Is CEA detectable in newborn puppies?

Yes. CEA can be detected in pups as young as 5 weeks old. Early screening is recommended because the mildest form can appear to resolve with age.

Do I need both an OFA eye exam and a DNA test?

Yes, for thorough screening. The OFA exam detects clinical signs, while the DNA test identifies carriers that show no symptoms but can pass the mutation.

How much does CEA testing cost?

An OFA eye exam costs about $50–$100 per dog. A DNA test costs $65–$150. Combined, a complete assessment for a parent or puppy is under $250.

What does "go normal" mean in CEA?

As the retina matures, the pigment layer may cover areas of mild choroidal hypoplasia, making the condition appear to resolve. This is why early puppy screening is critical.

Can two clear dogs produce an affected puppy?

No. If both parents are genetically clear (no copy of the mutation), there is zero chance of producing an affected puppy.

Is CEA more common in males or females?

Prevalence is equal between sexes because CEA is an autosomal recessive condition, not linked to the X or Y chromosome.

How do I find a breeder who screens for CEA?

Contact the American Shetland Sheepdog Association for breeder referrals. Always ask for OFA or CERF numbers and DNA test results. You can also search the OFA online database.

What should I do if my Sheltie suddenly starts bumping into things?

This may indicate retinal detachment or hemorrhage. Seek emergency veterinary care immediately. Do not wait for a scheduled appointment.

Does CEA affect both eyes equally?

Not always. Severity can differ between the two eyes. A dog may have mild CEA in one eye and a coloboma in the other.

Can CEA be cured or treated?

There is no cure for CEA. Management focuses on preventing complications such as retinal detachment through regular monitoring and avoiding activities that increase intraocular pressure.

How long does a CEA DNA test take?

Results typically arrive within 2 to 4 weeks after submitting a cheek swab or blood sample to an approved laboratory.

What other eye conditions should I test for in Shelties?

Progressive retinal atrophy, cataracts, and lens luxation also affect the breed. Annual comprehensive eye exams should include these checks.

Where can I share my dog's CEA test results?

Submit results to the OFA database. This information helps track breed health trends and assists prospective puppy buyers.
